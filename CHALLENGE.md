# The Challenge: Make One Person’s Hard Day Easier

## 1. The Brief

Build something that makes one person’s hard day meaningfully easier.

That is the challenge.

What that means, and what form your solution takes, is up to you.

For some teams, this may become a tool. For others, it may become an app, a workflow, an assistant, an interface, or something else entirely. The label does not matter. What matters is whether it helps.

This challenge is intentionally open. Different teams may interpret it in completely different ways. That is part of the point.

## 2. The Problem Space

Many people carry forms of daily pressure that are invisible to everyone else.

Sometimes it is administrative overload. Sometimes it is emotional labour. Sometimes it is fragmented communication, repetitive coordination, or a system that is confusing, slow, and always demanding attention. These pressures build quietly. Over time, they become exhausting.

A hard day does not have to be dramatic. Very often, it is made of small frictions repeated again and again.

Your job is to notice those frictions, understand them through a real person, and build something that genuinely reduces them.

## 3. What We Are Looking For

We are not looking for the biggest idea.

We are looking for a thoughtful one.

The strongest projects will be grounded in a real person, a real situation, and a clear understanding of what makes that situation difficult. The format of the solution is open. The standard is whether your work shows insight, care, usefulness, and strong execution.

A strong submission should answer questions like these:

1. Who is this for?
2. What makes their day hard?
3. What did you learn from speaking to them?
4. What did you build, and why?
5. How does it make their day easier in practice?

## 4. Your Interpretation Matters

This challenge is not about arriving at one correct format.

Two teams may start from the same prompt and build completely different things. Both can be excellent.

One team may focus on reducing admin. Another may focus on reducing confusion. Another may focus on helping someone make faster decisions, feel less overwhelmed, or recover time and mental energy.

You are being judged not on whether your project fits a predefined template, but on how clearly and convincingly you define the problem you chose to solve.

Your framing matters.

Your reasoning matters.

Your story matters.

## 5. Expectations

Your project should be:

1. Rooted in a real person or a clearly grounded user story
2. Built during the hackathon
3. Deployed and accessible live
4. Clear in what it does and why it matters

You do not need to build something massive.

You do need to build something intentional.

## 6. Rubric

Projects will be evaluated using the following rubric.

A strong project is not only well framed and useful. It is also well built. Judges will care about the quality of the technical execution, the ambition of what you attempted, and how effectively you used Cursor to go further than you otherwise could.

### 6.1 Understanding of the Person and Problem

How well did the team understand the person they are building for?

1. Is the problem clearly defined?
2. Does it feel grounded in a real situation?
3. Is there evidence of empathy, observation, or conversation?
4. Did the team identify something meaningful rather than generic?

### 6.2 Relevance and Usefulness

How meaningfully does the project improve that person’s day?

1. Does it solve a real friction point?
2. Is the value concrete rather than hypothetical?
3. Would the intended person actually find this helpful?
4. Does it reduce effort, time, confusion, stress, or cognitive load in a believable way?

### 6.3 Technical Execution

How strong is the project as a piece of technology?

1. Is the project functional, live, and reliable?
2. Is the core experience understandable and well implemented?
3. Does the technical build match the problem well?
4. Did the team make thoughtful product and engineering decisions?
5. Did they build something that feels polished enough to be used, not only shown?

### 6.4 Creativity and Interpretation

How thoughtfully did the team interpret the challenge?

1. Did they take an original angle?
2. Did they frame the challenge in a compelling way?
3. Does the project show creative thinking in the problem, the solution, or both?
4. Does it feel intentional rather than formulaic?

### 6.5 Use of Cursor and Technical Leverage

How well did the team use Cursor to extend what was possible?

1. Did they use Cursor in a meaningful way throughout the process?
2. Did Cursor help them explore ideas, build faster, solve technical problems, or ship something more ambitious?
3. Did they use the tools to raise the quality of the product rather than simply speed up the process?
4. Did they attempt something they likely would not have built in the same timeframe without Cursor?

### 6.6 Presentation and Storytelling

How clearly did the team explain what they built and why it matters?

1. Was the narrative easy to follow?
2. Did they make the person and the problem feel real?
3. Did they explain their decisions clearly?
4. Did the presentation strengthen the project rather than distract from it?

## 7. A Good Way to Think About It

A strong project usually does three things well.

1. It identifies a real source of friction.
2. It builds something focused enough to address it.
3. It explains clearly why that matters.

That is enough.

You do not need a huge product vision.

You do not need to solve everything.

You need to make one hard day easier in a way that feels real.

## 8. Final Note

The best projects usually begin with attention.

Talk to someone.

Listen carefully.

Notice what makes their day harder than it looks.

Then build from there.
